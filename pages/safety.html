Hazard Awareness in the Fabrication Lab

The Fabrication Lab involves various hazards that require controls to ensure safety. It is crucial to remain aware of these hazards and work safely.

Common Hazards in the Lab

Sharp Objects: The lab contains sharp objects such as metal chips, unfinished parts, blades on machines, and machine tool bits. Cut-resistant gloves are available for use.

Crushing / Pinching Hazards: These hazards can occur between moving machine parts, in the ways of a mill table, between a machine and a wall, or while using tools like pliers and wrenches.

Rotating Machinery: Hazards are present in mill and drill press spindles, as well as in grinders and hand drills.

Chemicals: Certain chemicals like lubricants, solvents, glues, and resins are used in the lab. Always check the container for warnings and read the Safety Data Sheet (SDS) for detailed information. Disposable safety gloves and other PPE are available.

Heavy Items: Be cautious of heavy items that can fall or crush hands if lifted incorrectly. When in doubt, request assistance for a two-person lift or the use of a hoist.

Hot Items: Hot items include recently processed parts and machines that have been in use. Wear heat-resistant PPE as needed.

Slip Hazards: Address slip hazards, caused by dust and spills, immediately with assistance.

Safety Equipment and Dress Code

Safety Glasses (with side protection) / Face Shield: All individuals in the Fabrication Lab must wear safety glasses marked Z87+ whenever a subtractive machine is running or when removing 3D printed support material. These glasses provide significant eye protection against unexpected flying particles and objects.

Hearing Protection: Hearing protection is required when the CNC Router is running.

Personal Dress: Proper dress is essential for safety. It lessens hazards related to rotating machinery, flying chips (metal, wood, plastic), and heavy objects. Ensure your dress meets these specifications:

  • No loose or dangling hair, clothing, or jewelry that might get caught in rotating/moving machinery.
  • Wear long pants to protect against sharp and hot flying chips.
  • Use closed-toed shoes with good grip and stability to prevent slips and falls.

Optional Gloves: Cut-resistant and disposable gloves are available for specific tasks. Cut-resistant gloves are used when handling sharp objects or during cutting procedures. Disposable gloves are optional for messy or dirty processes, handling paints, glues, etc. Always consult the safety data sheet.

No Open Food or Drink: Unsealed or uncovered food and drink are not allowed in the lab. The presence of loose metal chips and flying particles poses a risk of contamination and injury. Drinks can also create spill and slip hazards.

OSHA Sources Supporting Safety Measures

The information provided about hazard awareness in the Fabrication Lab aligns with various OSHA guidelines and standards. Here are some relevant OSHA sources that support the safety measures mentioned:

  • Personal Protective Equipment (PPE): OSHA has specific standards for personal protective equipment, which can be found in 29 CFR 1910 Subpart I. Employers are required to provide PPE to minimize exposure to hazards that cause serious workplace injuries and illnesses.
  • Machine Guarding: OSHA addresses machine guarding hazards in specific standards for general industry, maritime, and construction. These standards aim to protect workers from injuries caused by moving machine parts, such as crushing or pinching hazards.
  • Chemical Hazards: OSHA's Hazard Communication Standard (HCS), 29 CFR 1910.1200, ensures that employees are informed of the hazards associated with exposure to chemicals in the workplace. Employers must provide Safety Data Sheets (SDS) for hazardous chemicals, which contain information on proper handling, storage, and disposal.
  • Heavy Items: While there isn't a specific OSHA standard for lifting heavy items, general safety guidelines recommend using proper lifting techniques and seeking assistance when needed to prevent injuries.
  • Hot Items: OSHA's general industry standards, such as 29 CFR 1910 Subpart Q for welding, cutting, and brazing, address the hazards associated with hot items and the need for appropriate PPE.
  • Slip Hazards: OSHA's general industry standards, such as 29 CFR 1910 Subpart D for walking-working surfaces, address slip hazards and the need for proper housekeeping and maintenance to prevent slips, trips, and falls.
  • Safety Equipment and Dress Code: OSHA requires employers to provide appropriate PPE, including safety glasses, face shields, and hearing protection, depending on the specific hazards present in the workplace. Additionally, OSHA has guidelines for proper attire to minimize the risk of injury.
